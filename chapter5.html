Rank & Linear Dependence

Ch 4 solved our loan model — but what if income and age are highly correlated (older applicants tend to earn more)? Then one feature is just a scaled copy of the other, XᵀX becomes singular, and the Normal Equation breaks. Drag the slider to see exactly when and why.

What does "rank" mean?

The rank of a matrix is the number of linearly independent rows (or columns) — rows that cannot be written as combinations of others.

  • Full rank: all columns independent → unique solution → Normal Equation works ✓
  • Rank deficient: some columns are combinations of others → no unique solution → Normal Equation fails ✗

As the two features become identical, two columns of X become identical, making two rows of XᵀX become proportional. The matrix becomes singular.

Rank in terms of row operations: Rank = number of non-zero rows after row reduction (REF). A singular matrix has at least one entirely zero row in its REF — exactly what you see when you row-reduce a rank-deficient XᵀX.

Practical consequences in ML

Situation Matrix rank Effect on model
All features independentFull rankUnique θ — model learns correctly
Two features highly correlatedNearly rank deficientθ unstable, large values, high variance
Duplicate column (r=1)Rank deficientNormal Equation fails — infinite solutions
More features than samplesRank = m < nUnderdetermined — overfit perfectly
Fix: Regularisation (Ridge Regression) Add λI to XᵀX: solve (XᵀX + λI)θ = Xᵀy. Since λ > 0, the matrix is always full rank — invertible even with correlated features. This is the most common practical solution.
Story so far: Correlated features (income & age) can break the Normal Equation by reducing matrix rank. Regularisation helps — but inverting large matrices is expensive regardless.
