The Normal Distribution

In Ch 9, income emerged as a strong predictor. But how do we describe its distribution mathematically? The Normal (Gaussian) distribution is the most important shape in statistics — and it underlies assumptions in almost every ML model.

Z-scores for our 8 applicants

A z-score answers: "how many standard deviations from the mean is this applicant?" z < −1 = unusually low income. z > +1 = unusually high income.

The 68–95–99.7 rule

For any Normal distribution, the same percentage of data always falls within 1σ, 2σ, 3σ of the mean. This is why σ is so useful — it's a universal measuring stick.

within 1σ of mean
(μ−σ to μ+σ)
95%
within 2σ of mean
(μ−2σ to μ+2σ)
99.7%
within 3σ of mean
(μ−3σ to μ+3σ)
Why this matters for the loan model: When we later compute probabilities (Ch 11–13), we'll assume features are roughly Gaussian. Z-scores also let us normalise features — subtract mean, divide by σ — so that income and age are on the same scale. This makes gradient descent converge faster (Ch 16).
