What ML is Trying to Do

In Ch 1 we structured our loan applicant data as matrix X and target y. Now: what does the ML algorithm actually do with it? It finds the line that best separates defaulters (1) from repayers (0) by minimising prediction error. Drag the sliders and see this goal in action — and notice a limitation we'll fix later.

The data — Income vs Default

Each dot is one applicant. X-axis = income ($k), Y-axis = default label (1 = defaulted, 0 = repaid). The blue line is our model's prediction. Drag the sliders to move the line and watch the error change.

Prediction errors (residuals)

A residual is the difference between the actual label (0 or 1) and what our line predicts. The vertical red lines on the plot show these errors. ML minimises the Sum of Squared Residuals (SSR).

Why square the errors? Squaring makes all errors positive (so negative and positive errors don't cancel), and penalises large errors more heavily. The line that minimises SSR is the Ordinary Least Squares solution — the foundation of linear regression.

What the line actually is

The line is the equation:   ŷ = 1.50 − 0.020 · income

θ₀ (intercept) is the baseline risk score.
θ₁ (slope) says: "each extra $k of income decreases the predicted default score by θ₁."

⚠ Spot the problem: The line can predict values > 1 (impossible for a probability) or < 0. For a 0/1 target, a straight line is the wrong tool — we need something that stays in [0, 1]. That's exactly what the sigmoid function in Chapter 11 provides.
The ML goal in one sentence: Find the values of θ that make SSR = Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² as small as possible. The next chapters show how linear algebra solves this exactly.
Story so far: ML's job is to find parameters θ that minimise prediction error. We've seen this visually.
