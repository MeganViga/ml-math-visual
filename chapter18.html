ROC Curve & AUC

In Ch 17 we evaluated the model at one threshold. But how do we compare two different models regardless of threshold? The ROC curve plots true positive rate vs false positive rate across every possible threshold. The AUC (area under the curve) is a single number that summarises model quality.

What the ROC curve plots

Y-axis: TPR (Recall)

= TP/(TP+FN). What fraction of actual defaults did we catch? Higher = better for the bank.

X-axis: FPR

= FP/(FP+TN). What fraction of good customers did we incorrectly decline? Lower = better.

As threshold decreases: more applicants are flagged as defaulters → TPR rises (catch more defaults) but also FPR rises (more false alarms). The ROC curve traces this tradeoff.

Why AUC matters for the loan model: AUC = probability that a randomly chosen defaulter gets a higher risk score than a randomly chosen repayer. AUC is threshold-independent — it tells you how well the model ranks applicants, regardless of where you set the cutoff. A bank can use AUC to compare models and then choose the threshold based on business cost.
Story so far: ROC + AUC gives us a threshold-independent view of model quality. Our loan model has reasonable AUC — much better than random.
