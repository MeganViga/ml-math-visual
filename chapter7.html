PCA — Finding the Best Axes

In Ch 5 we saw that correlated features (income & age) can make XᵀX singular. PCA asks a deeper question: if income and age carry similar information, can we merge them into one new axis that captures most of the variance? That axis is the first eigenvector of XᵀX.

Why do we need new axes?

Think of our two loan applicant features: income ($k) and age. In our dataset, higher-income applicants tend to be older — so most variation lies along a diagonal between the two axes, not along either axis alone. PCA finds that diagonal and makes it the new X-axis.

  • PC1 — the direction of maximum variance (captures the income+age trend)
  • PC2 — perpendicular to PC1 (captures residual variation)
  • Project data onto PC1 only → 2 correlated features compressed to 1, minimal information loss

PCA and linear algebra: Eigenvectors of XᵀX are the principal components. Eigenvalues tell you how much variance each component captures. This is exactly the same XᵀX from the Normal Equation — repurposed for a different goal.
In deep learning: PCA is used for preprocessing (de-correlate inputs), visualising high-dimensional embeddings (e.g. word vectors in 2D), and understanding what a neural network has learned.
