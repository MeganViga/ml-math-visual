Maximum Likelihood Estimation

In Ch 11 we can compute P(default) for any θ. But which θ is best? Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) answers: find the θ that makes the outcomes we actually observed as probable as possible. This is the probabilistic justification for how logistic regression learns.

The idea: "which θ best explains what we saw?"

For each training applicant, we observed their outcome (defaulted/repaid). A good model should assign high probability to what actually happened.

  • Applicant 1 defaulted → we want P(default) to be high
  • Applicant 2 repaid → we want P(default) to be low
  • MLE: multiply all these probabilities together → maximise the product
  • In practice: take log (log-likelihood) so we sum instead of multiply

Why this matters for the loan model: MLE gives us a principled way to learn θ: not by minimising a geometric error (like SSR in Ch 2), but by maximising probability. As we'll see in Ch 13, taking the negative log-likelihood gives us the cross-entropy loss — the loss function used to train logistic regression.
Story so far: MLE defines what good θ means for a classification model: the θ that makes our observed training outcomes most probable. Maximising log-likelihood is equivalent to minimising cross-entropy loss.
