Derivatives — The Slope of the Loss

In Ch 13 we have a loss function L(θ). To minimise it, we need to know which direction is "downhill". The derivative dL/dθ answers that — it is the instantaneous slope of L at any point. Gradient descent (Ch 16) is entirely built on this one idea.

What a derivative tells us

dL/dθ > 0 (positive slope)
Loss increases as θ increases. To reduce loss, move θ to the left (decrease θ).
dL/dθ < 0 (negative slope)
Loss decreases as θ increases. To reduce loss, move θ to the right (increase θ).
dL/dθ = 0
We're at a minimum (or maximum). Gradient descent stops here — this is the optimal θ.
|dL/dθ| large
Steep slope — we're far from the minimum. Take a bigger step (but not too big!).

The chain rule — how ∂L/∂θ is computed

Cross-entropy loss flows through several functions. The chain rule lets us differentiate through all of them:

L = −log(σ(z))
∂L/∂z = σ(z) − y  (sigmoid gradient — elegant!)
∂L/∂θⱼ = (σ(z) − y) · xⱼ  (chain rule: multiply by ∂z/∂θⱼ = xⱼ)

This says: the gradient for weight θⱼ is proportional to the prediction error × the feature value. If income is 35k and we predicted 0.8 but the actual is 0 (repaid), the error is 0.8 and we reduce θ₁ by 0.8 × 35 × α.

Why this matters for the loan model: The gradient ∂L/∂θ₁ tells us exactly how to adjust the income weight to better predict defaults. Features with large values (like income in $k) will produce large gradients — this is why normalising features (Ch 10 z-scores) is important before training.
